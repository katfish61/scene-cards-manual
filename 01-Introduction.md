# Chapter 1 — Introduction

## 1.1 What Scene Cards Is

Scene Cards is a digital scene-card wall for film and television productions.
It was conceived to replace the paper index cards that a production office pins to a corkboard
during prep, and keeps working through the shoot and into post.

Each scene in your script becomes a card on a digital wall. These cards can carry
everything a department typically needs for that scene.

- Scene number, episode, location, a one-line brief
- A still or concept image
- Reference files — photos, PDFs, videos, location scouts, storyboards, camera plans, any file type
- The raw script text for the scene
- Revision colour, omission state, comments
- Shoot-day assignment
- Sound, camera and continuity report entries

The wall itself has three modes:

| Mode         | Purpose                                                                 |
|--------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Script**   | Scene order as it reads — the default view for breakdowns and story work |
| **Location** | Group scenes by location for scouting and scheduling                     |
| **Schedule** | Arranged by shoot day once a one-liner is imported                       |

Scene Cards runs on macOS and iPadOS. Your document is a single
`.scenecards` file that travels with you — iCloud Drive, AirDrop, or a USB
stick all work.

## 1.2 What Scene Cards Isn't

Knowing the limits up front saves frustration later.

- **Not a scheduling engine.** Scene Cards reads a one-liner PDF and lays the
  wall out by shoot day, but it doesn't generate a schedule from scratch. Use
  Movie Magic Scheduling, StudioBinder or similar for board-building; bring
  the resulting one-liner in.
- **Not a screenwriting app.** You can import PDF and Final Draft (`.fdx`)
  scripts and read them inside the app, but Scene Cards is not an editor —
  revisions happen in your writing tool of choice.
- **Not a call sheet generator.** You can attach call sheets to shoot days for
  reference, but generating them is outside scope.
- **Not a real-time collaboration tool.** Scene Cards documents are
  single-user files. Sharing is handled by passing the document between team
  members (see §14 *Collaborating on a Production*).

## 1.3 Who Should Use It

Scene Cards was designed around the daily workflows of the crew members who
live inside the breakdown and the schedule. You'll find it most useful if you
are a:

| Role                              | Typical use                                              |
|-----------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| **Producer / UPM**                | High-level overview across episodes, prep planning       |
| **1st AD**                        | Scheduling, day-by-day reordering, revision tracking     |
| **Script Supervisor**             | Daily continuity logging, per-scene notes and photos     |
| **Sound Mixer**                   | Daily sound report storage, per-scene entries, wild tracks, playback etc. |
| **DIT / Camera Dept.**            | Camera report storage, slate references, camera layouts  |
| **Director / DoP**                | Reference gathering, location scouts, visual planning    |
| **Production Designer / Art Dept.** | Location and scene reference boards, prop photos, set drawings. |
| **Picture Editor / Assistant**    | Pull stills and references for post; print scene cards; review shoot-day reports in Schedule mode |

If you're none of the above but still need to organise a shoot by scene —
student films, theatre, documentary — Scene Cards will still do the job.

## 1.4 System Requirements

Scene Cards requires one of the following:

- **macOS** Ventura 13.5 or later silicon or Intel
- **iPadOS** 16.6

Recommended:

- 4 GB of free disk space per active production (stills, references and
  reports add up fast)
- iCloud Drive enabled if you want documents to follow you between devices
- A stylus is useful on iPad for annotating references, but is not required

> ⚠ **Caution** — a single `.scenecards` document can hold hundreds of scenes
> and many gigabytes of attached media. On iPadOS, keep an eye on local
> storage if you're working offline.

## 1.5 Installation

### 1.5.1 🍎 macOS

1. Open the Mac App Store.
2. Search for **Scene Cards**.
3. Click **Get**, then **Install**.
4. Launch Scene Cards from the Applications folder or Launchpad.

### 1.5.2 📐 iPadOS

1. Open the App Store.
2. Search for **Scene Cards**.
3. Tap **Get**, authenticate, and wait for the download to complete.
4. Launch Scene Cards from your Home Screen or App Library.


## 1.6 First Launch

### 1.6.1 🍎 macOS

On first launch, Scene Cards opens the standard **Open a Document** dialog.
You have three choices:

- **New Document** — create an empty wall, 8 cards wide by default.
- **Open Existing** — pick a `.scenecards` package from disk or iCloud.
- **Recents** — resume a document you've worked on before.

### 1.6.2 📐 iPadOS

On first launch, iPadOS shows a **Welcome** screen introducing the three wall
modes. Tap **Continue** to proceed to the Files browser, where you can:

- Tap **Create Document** to start a new wall, 6 cards wide by default.
- Tap an existing `.scenecards` document to open it.

> ✱ **Tip** — the Welcome screen only appears on first run. To revisit it
> later, see §17 *Settings & Preferences*.

## 1.7 Activation and Subscription

Scene Cards offers annual

## 1.8 Conventions Used in This Manual

The following conventions appear throughout this manual. Familiarise yourself
with them now.

**Platform badges** — where a procedure differs by platform, the relevant
badge appears alongside the heading:

- 🍎 macOS
- 📐 iPadOS

**Callout boxes** — used for information that sits outside the main flow:

> ⓘ **Note** — supplementary information you may want to know.

> ✱ **Tip** — a shortcut or best-practice suggestion.

> ⚠ **Caution** — loss of data or time is possible if ignored.

> 🔒 **Permission required** — the action requires a macOS or iPadOS
> permission prompt.

**Keyboard notation** — modifier keys use Apple symbols: ⌘ (Command),
⌥ (Option), ⌃ (Control), ⇧ (Shift). Key combinations are joined with no
separator: `⌘S` means hold Command and press S.

**Menu paths** — file menu navigation uses `→`: `File → New` means open the
File menu, then choose New.

**Placeholder filenames** — where examples need a filename, we use
`MyProduction.scenecards` unless otherwise noted.
